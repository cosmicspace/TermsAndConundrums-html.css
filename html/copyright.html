Copyright

What is Copyright?

Whenever you see a message like this:

Copyright © 2015 Matt Sessa, Brendan Blanchard and Jason Armstrong.

All rights reserved.

then you've seen a Copyright notice.


Copyright is the idea that you are empowered as a content creator to have certain rights. If you write a song, code a program or create any other work, Copyright law ensures that someone else cannot just copy what you made and portray it to everyone without your permission. Without this understanding (and law!), content creators would have no incentive to create anything, since they would not be able to benefit from it before someone else could just take away any benefits of their work. This would lead to the larger issue with society that no works are produced, since no benefit comes from them.


When this should be used

Copyright offers the widest range of protections for the creator, and typically inhibits others from using their work. Often, this is the default choice for a content creator, since it ensures that their work will not be used without their permission, and any demands for payment must be respected to use the work.


In fact, licensing works against Copyright in some ways since it is used to grant some of these rights to others so that work on them can continue without the creator's needing to offer their input or permissions, which would be necessary in order for someone else to contribute to or modify the work.


Copyright is rather automatic and technically doesn't need anything to be done by the work's creator. Furthermore, it However, it would be a good idea to register one's work with the Copyright Office in order to ensure that they can continue to have the rights of being a creator.

Length or term of license

Length of license corresponds to the work’s copyright. In the USA, this is typically 70 years after the death of the creator, or 120 after it was made by an organization, at which point, the work becomes public domain.

Real world examples

  • Most for sale software packages
  • Virtually all physical books for sale

History and shenanigans

Much of the fanfare for licensing options goes against Copyright in that Copyright is so restrictive. There are advantages to this but Copyright doesn't work for a number of projects, especially Open Source Software. Despite this, most content is protected by Copyright, and negotiations with the Copyright holder must commence before a creator's work can be used by others. Otherwise, the content creator could sue subsequent users, modifiers and distributors of their content.
